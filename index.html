About Me

I am a second-year Ph.D. student studying operations research in the Operations Research and Industrial Engineering Program (ORIE) at the University of Texas at Austin. My current work explores the Food-Energy-Water nexus through the lens of disaster resilience and planning. I am an NSF NRT Fellow as part of the INFEWS scholars program and a Stanford University Macro-Energy Systems Fellow. I serve on both the UT Graduate Student Assembly and Mechanical Engineering Graduate Student Board as an ORIE representative. I am the current President of UTâ€™s INFORMS Student Chapter.

I earned an M.S. degree in operations research at the University of Maryland. My M.S. advisor was Dr. Steven Gabriel.. Our team, in collaboration with our corporate partner, Whisker Labs aimed to bring optimal demand-side management to the residential sector with the aid of internet-connected thermostats. My work focused on using dynamic programming to help Retail Electric Providers (REPs) with optimal demand response scheduling.

In my spare time, I enjoy hiking, cooking, and most of all, making pottery. I love the squish of mud under my hands, the excitment guiding it into wares of all shapes and sizes. And of course, it's always fun to get a little dirty!

Research

Using Dynamic Programming to Optimize End-User Electricity Use

Graduate Research Assistant • August 2017 - May 2019
Advisor: Dr. Steven A. Gabriel

Price volatility in electricity markets can cause Retail Electric Providers significant losses in short amounts of time. Along with our corporate partner Whisker Labs we have been using internet-connected thermostats to remotely shift residential HVAC load from periods of high prices to low prices. My work has two thrusts: first to predict price spikes, and second to optimally shift demand away from high prices. The first step involves calibrating Recurrent Neural Networks to predict energy price spikes for real-time decision making. The second step uses Stochastic Dynamic Programming (SDP) to optimally schedule residential demand response events.

Modeling Harmful Algal Blooms in the Chesapeake Bay

Undergraduate Research Assistant• September 2016 - May 2017
Advisor: Dr. Kaye Brubaker

Harmful Algal Blooms (HABs) pose a threat to both ecosystem and human health. The dangers of these organisms are twofold: many strains produce toxins lethal to fish and harmful to humans, and the decay of algae biomass can cause eutrophication in water bodies. For this project, I first developed a life cycle model of algae bloom probabilities on the Chesapeake Bay. Then, I trained and validated model using cell abundances provided by the MD Department of Natural Resources. This project was completed as fufilment of requirements of the Engineering Honors Program and earned the Most Outstanding Research Award among other projects completed that year.
